    <h1 class="text-center">Story Points Aren&#x27;t the Problem</h1>
    <div class="flex flex-col min-h-screen">
        <article>
            

<div class="prose-sm italic">
    <div class="flex flex-col sm:flex-row justify-evenly items-center text-sm gap-y-1">

<p>Like many developers, I hate story pointing.</p>
<p>It feels like I’ve wasted <em>years</em> of my life in sprint planning sessions that spiral into arguments over whether something is a 2 or a 3. I’ve also sat through too many sprint planning sessions watching teams treat story points as time estimates, and walk away with no more clarity than when they started.</p>
<p>It all feels likes a performance that gives <del>management</del> everyone a false sense of control.</p>
<p>In fact, some people just do away with story points altogether, and I get it. I was once in that camp too.</p>
<p>But, what I if told you</p>
<blockquote>
<p>story points aren't the problem.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>I’ve noticed three common mistakes that make story points and the whole sprint planning exercise feel like a giant waste of time. Let's see what they are and how we can fix them to make story-pointing not suck.</p>
<h2 id="mistake-1-treating-story-points-as-a-proxy-for-time">Mistake #1: Treating Story Points as a Proxy for Time</h2>
<p>How often have you heard this in a sprint planning session -</p>
<blockquote>
<p>This should take 1 week, so… five story points?</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That’s not how story points work. They’re not about time.</p>
<p>A 3-point story might take one day or more than a week. I know you dont want to hear it but it doesn't matter.</p>
<p>Story points should reflect the <strong>complexity</strong> of the task.</p>
<p>Here’s a better way to think about them:</p>
<h3 id="point">1 Point</h3>
<p><strong>Potential Traits:</strong> No unknowns, low risk, minimal code changes.</p>
<p><strong>Examples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Adding logs to existing code</li>
<li>Trivial changes behind a feature flag</li>
<li>Cleaning up a simple feature flag</li>
<li>Mechanical refactoring</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="points">2 Points</h3>
<p><strong>Potential Traits:</strong> Low risk, some minor unknowns, small test updates.</p>
<p><strong>Examples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Simple changes behind a feature flag</li>
<li>Creating a new dashboard with existing data</li>
<li>Cleaning up a complex feature flag</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="points-1">3 Points</h3>
<p><strong>Potential Traits:</strong> Some unknowns, moderate risk, might require coordination with others.</p>
<p><strong>Examples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Fixing a bug with a known root cause</li>
<li>New functionality that can be feature flagged cleanly</li>
<li>Work that involves coordination with another developer</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="points-2">5 Points</h3>
<p><strong>Potential traits:</strong> Some unknowns, large code changes, lots of new tests, external dependencies</p>
<p><strong>Examples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Technical investigation for new features or bugs</li>
<li>Work that's difficult to feature flag</li>
<li>Cross-team coordination</li>
</ul>
<p>These are good candidates to split into smaller tickets once the investigation is done.</p>
<h3 id="points-3">8 Points</h3>
<p><strong>Potential traits:</strong> Many unknowns, risky changes, hard to estimate confidently.</p>
<p>If you’re estimating something at 8 points, you probably need a spike first. Timebox it, then use what you’ve learned to create smaller, pointable stories.</p>
<p><img src="/assets/images/posts/story-points-arent-problem/infographic.png" alt="Storypoint Infographic" /></p>
<p><em>Note: You can download the full sized infographic from <a href="/assets/images/posts/story-points-arent-problem/infographic_original.png">Printable Infographic</a></em></p>
<h2 id="mistake-2-estimating-in-isolation">Mistake #2: Estimating in Isolation</h2>
<p>Ever had a product owner or a tech lead show up to planning with stories already estimated?</p>
<p>Estimation is a team sport. Different people catch different risks, assumptions, and edge cases. Getting everyone involved builds shared understanding and better team cohesion.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The number matters less than the conversation.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Here’s what to do:</p>
<ul>
<li>The feature lead fleshes out the ticket ahead of planning.</li>
<li>They give a 30-second overview during the session and answer questions.
<ul>
<li>No need to dive into every detail - but enough for the team to understand the <strong>why</strong>, the <strong>approach</strong>, and have the <strong>confidence</strong> to estimate complexity.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Then use Planning Poker to uncover different perspectives.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="mistake-3-arguing-over-2-vs-3-points">Mistake #3: Arguing Over 2 vs 3 Points</h2>
<p><img src="/assets/images/posts/story-points-arent-problem/two_vs_three.png" alt="two vs three" /></p>
<p>You know the moment. One person says it’s a 2. Another insists it’s a 3. Suddenly, you’re 20 minutes deep into a conversation that doesn’t matter.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>You’re optimizing for precision in a system designed for approximation.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That’s not just wasteful — it completely misses the point (pun unintended).</p>
<p><strong>Timebox these debates.</strong> If you can't agree in 1-2 minutes, pick the higher number and move on. It's not worth derailing the whole session.</p>
<p>However, if there’s a big delta — say, someone thinks it’s a 2 and someone else thinks it’s a 5 — that is worth unpacking. Someone’s probably missing something.</p>
<hr />
<h2 id="final-thoughts">Final Thoughts</h2>
<p>Story points aren't about precision. They're about shared clarity.</p>
<p>Used well, they build shared context. Used poorly, they fuel endless debates.</p>
<p>So next time you're estimating ask yourself:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Do we actually understand the scope, complexity, and purpose of this work?</p>
</blockquote>
<p>If the answer’s yes, the number doesn't matter.</p>
<p><strong>What’s your biggest frustration with story points — and how have you worked around it?</strong></p>
