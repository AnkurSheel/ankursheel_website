<html><head></head><body><p>Every week I come across some great articles. Here are some of the posts I feel you will enjoy reading as much as I did.</p>
<p>Do you know what Choice Architecture is? It's a term to describe how the way you present choices to consumers can affect their decision making. <strong>Shawn Wang</strong> shows <a href="https://www.swyx.io/choice-architecture/">how both gyms and startups do choice architecture</a>.</p>
<p><strong>David Perrel</strong> explains his <a href="https://www.perell.com/tweetstorms/cribs-my-writing-feedback-formula">CRIB system to give writing feedback</a>. Regardless of whether you have to provide feedback to someone else, applying it to your writing and speaking will help a lot.</p>
<p><strong>Jamie Wong</strong> shares a fun and highly well-written story about <a href="http://jamie-wong.com/post/debugging-misadventures/">debugging with examples</a> taken from some of the bugs he has seen at Figma.</p>
<p>An insightful read by <strong>Kyle Gawley</strong> about what <a href="https://blog.usegravity.app/the-startup-success-delusion-that-put-me-in-hospital/">it is like to run a startup and how there is no one way to have a successful business</a>. He starts his story with the experience of passing out and how that scare led him to change his life and build a successful business as a digital nomad.</p>
<p>It's challenging to work on side projects when you have a full-time job and a family. <strong>Catalin Pit</strong> gives an insight into his daily schedule and how <a href="https://catalins.tech/how-do-i-balance-work-with-my-side-projects">he balances his side projects and content creation with his full-time job</a>.</p>
<p>We generally know the risks that we take, but in the end, it's only the tail end consequences that matter. <strong>Morgan Housel</strong> shares a personal story about <a href="https://www.collaborativefund.com/blog/the-three-sides-of-risk/">how his risk tolerance plunged after paying the ultimate price in a skiing accident</a>.</p>
<p>Our psychological default is to remember the negative stuff that happens in a day and complain. But, the truth is that for many people, more positive things happen in a day than negative. <strong>Jason Lengstorf</strong> explains how he <a href="https://lengstorf.com/how-to-be-positive/">reprogrammed his brain to become aware of his negativity and list the day's high points instead</a></p>
<p>Hope you enjoy reading these articles as much as I did.</p>
