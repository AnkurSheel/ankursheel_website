<html><head></head><body><p>Every week I come across some great articles. Here are some of the posts I feel you will enjoy reading as much as I did.</p>
<p>Asking good questions is hard, but it is a super important skill. <strong>Julia Evans</strong> has a great list of things that can help you <a href="https://jvns.ca/blog/good-questions/">ask better questions and get the answer you want</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Blake Boles</strong> explains a few <a href="https://www.blakeboles.com/2012/05/dreamfunding/">strategies for Crowdfunding</a>. For me, the key takeaway was how to decide what perks to give.</p>
<p>Most people have several lists they maintain. But have you heard of "The Suck List"? <strong>Mike Crittenden</strong> shares his thoughts on <a href="https://critter.blog/2018/06/11/the-power-of-the-suck-list/">why making a Suck list is helpful</a> and his process for creating one.</p>
<p>There is a fair bit of profanity in the article, but <strong>Charity Majors</strong> explains why <a href="https://charity.wtf/2017/05/11/the-engineer-manager-pendulum/">becoming a manager is not a promotion</a> and why engineers should make a practice of going back and forth between the engineering and manager roles.</p>
<p>Writing understandable code is an art. In this whimsical article, <strong>Robert Heaton</strong> takes a few examples and shows how <a href="https://robertheaton.com/2014/06/20/code-review-without-your-eyes/">you can tell a lot about the design of the code without actually reading any of it</a>.</p>
<p>How often have you heard "At our company, we value <code>Strong Opinions, Loosely Held</code>"? I have been a big proponent of this ideology because it lets the best ideas bubble up. <strong>Michael Natkin</strong> explains <a href="https://blog.glowforge.com/strong-opinions-loosely-held-might-be-the-worst-idea-in-tech/">why the "Strong Opinions, Loosely held" mantra is less than ideal</a> and what we can do instead.</p>
<p>Hope you enjoy reading these articles as much as I did.</p>
